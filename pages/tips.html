How to Stay Safe Online

You don't need to be a tech expert to protect yourself. These practical steps cover the most common ways people get compromised — and how to stop it.

Top Security Tips

Reusing passwords is the single biggest mistake most people make. If one site is breached (and they get breached all the time), attackers immediately try those same credentials on Gmail, banking, and social media — a technique called credential stuffing.

  • Use a password manager like Bitwarden (free) or 1Password to generate and store unique passwords.
  • You only need to remember one master password — the manager handles everything else.
  • Aim for passwords that are at least 12 characters, with a mix of letters, numbers, and symbols.

Two-factor authentication means that even if someone gets your password, they still can't log in without a second code — usually sent to your phone or generated by an app.

  • Authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Authy) are more secure than SMS codes.
  • Enable 2FA on your email first — it's the "master key" to most of your other accounts.
  • Most major services (Google, Apple, Facebook, banks) support 2FA in their security settings.

Phishing is when someone pretends to be a trusted organisation (your bank, Amazon, HMRC) to trick you into giving up your password or payment details. It's the most common way people get hacked.

  • Always check the sender's actual email address — not just the display name.
  • Hover over links before clicking. The URL shown at the bottom of your browser should match the company's real domain.
  • Legitimate organisations will never ask for your password via email.
  • When in doubt, go directly to the website by typing the address yourself.

Software updates aren't just adding new features — most of them patch security vulnerabilities that attackers actively exploit. Delaying updates leaves a known hole open.

  • Turn on automatic updates for your operating system, browser, and apps.
  • This applies to your phone too — iOS and Android updates frequently include security fixes.
  • Old, unsupported software (like Windows 7) no longer receives patches and should be replaced.

If you find your data in a breach, don't panic — but do act quickly. The damage is usually limited if you respond within a few days.

  • Change your password immediately on the affected service, and any site where you used the same password.
  • Check your email account for any suspicious logins or forwarding rules that were set up without your knowledge.
  • Enable 2FA if you haven't already.
  • If financial information was exposed, contact your bank and monitor your statements for unusual transactions.
  • Report identity theft to Action Fraud (UAE): 911.
